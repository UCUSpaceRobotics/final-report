\subsection{Geological Characterization of the Region}
\label{sec:geological_characterization}

\subsubsection{General Characterization}
\label{sec:general_characterization}
The study area lies in the eastern part of Hellas Planitia, roughly between \ang{37.2}--\ang{37.8} S and \ang{88.1}--\ang{89.4} E. The absolute surface elevation, based on MOLA data, is approximately \qty{-4250}{\meter}. Hellas Planitia is Mars's largest impact basin, formed during the Early Noachian. The region belongs to the eNhm geological unit (Early Noachian highland massif) according to the USGS classification.
The present-day climate is cold and dry. According to THEMIS data, night-time surface temperatures can drop to approximately \qty{111}{\kelvin} (\qty{-162}{\celsius}), and the current relief is dominated mainly by aeolian activity.
The geological history of the region includes basin formation, ancient highland crust development, intense cratering, Dao Vallis incision, and subsequent modification by aeolian processes.

\subsubsection{Scientific Potential of the Region}
\label{sec:scientific_potential}
The region has significant scientific potential because it combines ancient Noachian crust, one of the largest outflow channels on Mars, and geological units of different ages that record several stages of surface evolution.
The presence of Dao Vallis indicates extensive past fluvial activity and makes the region a valuable target for studying the role of liquid water in shaping the Martian landscape.
\textbf{The geological map in \autoref{fig:regional_geology_map_placeholder} shows units of different origins and ages within a compact area, creating strong conditions for studying the interaction between impact, fluvial, structural, and aeolian processes.}

\subsection{Landing Site Selection Rationale}
\label{sec:landing_site_selection}

\subsubsection{Candidate Analysis}
\label{sec:candidate_analysis}
Five potential landing ellipses were analyzed to select the landing site. The assessment used four criteria: landing safety, rover passability, access to geologically interesting terrain, and the ability to test the mission hypothesis.

\subsubsection{Final Landing Ellipse Selection}
\label{sec:final_landing_ellipse}
Among the five candidates, four met the engineering constraints for a safe landing, but most were dominated by homogeneous flat terrain or were located too far from features that would allow a meaningful test of the water--structural relationship.
The selected landing ellipse provides the best compromise between safety and scientific return. Its average slope is approximately \qty{1.59}{\degree}, the maximum slope is \qty{5.2}{\degree}, and the elevation difference inside the ellipse is \qty{228}{\meter}. This ellipse offers access to the most relevant geological targets within the required traverse range and therefore maximizes both operational safety and scientific value.

\subsection{Mission Scientific Hypothesis}
\label{sec:scientific_hypothesis}
\textcolor{ercblue}{\textbf{The working hypothesis is that Dao Vallis formed through the action of liquid water, and that the present-day topography of the surrounding area was subsequently modified by structural processes associated with the geological evolution of the Hellas Planitia basin.}}
If this hypothesis is correct, surface observations should reveal morphological evidence of water erosion, structures indicative of crustal deformation or collapse, and the relative sequence in which these features formed. The results will allow the relative roles of fluvial and structural processes in shaping the present-day relief to be assessed.

\subsection{Geological Investigation Plan}
\label{sec:investigation_plan}

\subsubsection{Selection of Regions of Interest}
\label{sec:roi_selection}
To test the mission hypothesis, three regions of interest were selected: the \textbf{pit chain}, \textbf{Dao Vallis} as the main \textbf{outflow channel} target, and \textbf{Apia crater}. The first location will be used to determine whether the aligned depressions are related to structural deformation or surface collapse, the second to investigate evidence of fluvial erosion, and the third because impact excavation may expose older subsurface materials, enabling comparison with surrounding geological units and reconstruction of the relative geological history of the area.

\subsubsection{Route Plan}
\label{sec:route_plan}
The rover will travel approximately \qty{27.5}{\kilo\meter} over about \num{472} sols, starting from the landing site and visiting the three ROIs in an order that minimizes travel distance while maximizing scientific return. The planned route first investigates the \textbf{pit chain}, then \textbf{Dao Vallis} as the \textbf{outflow channel}, and finally \textbf{Apia crater}. This sequence follows the scientific objectives of the mission and provides the observations required to evaluate the proposed geological hypothesis.

\subsection{Expected Results}
\label{sec:expected_results}
The mission is expected to determine whether Dao Vallis was primarily formed by liquid-water erosion and to assess the contribution of fluvial processes to the evolution of the eastern Hellas Planitia region. The observations will improve the geological interpretation of the study area, refine the understanding of the interaction between water-related and structural processes, and provide evidence to support or reject the proposed mission hypothesis.
These observations could also contribute to broader interpretations of the geological evolution of large Martian impact basins.
